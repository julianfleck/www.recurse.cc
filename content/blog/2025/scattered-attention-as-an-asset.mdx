# Attention as Commodity

Our relationship with attention is undergoing a fundamental transformation, and not everyone is happy about it. In an age of unprecedented information complexity, our minds have evolved patterns of thinking that are increasingly pathologized as deficits.

> **The pathologization of scattered attention reflects our tendency to frame cognitive adaptations through the lens of historical ideals rather than present needs. What we label as deficits may be emergent capabilities for navigating an increasingly complex information landscape.**

The traditional ideal of sustained, singular focus — the monk-like ability to concentrate deeply on one task for hours — is increasingly presented as the “correct” way to think, with any deviation viewed as a pathology to be corrected. We’re told that our ancestors possessed powers of concentration that we’ve somehow lost to the distractions of the digital age.

Yet this narrative deserves scrutiny. Think about how you actually process information in your daily life: rarely in neat, sequential packages, but rather in bursts of insight, fragments of understanding, pieces that somehow feel connected but don’t yet have the bridges built between them. We jump between contexts, make unexpected connections, and somehow weave meaning from what might appear as chaos to an outside observer.

Interestingly, this shift reveals something counterintuitive about human cognition: sometimes we need a degree of “noise” to achieve focus. Consider how many people work better in cafes than in silent rooms, or how white noise machines have become essential tools for concentration. This seemingly paradoxical relationship between distraction and focus hints at deeper truths about how our minds actually work.

# The Technological Reshaping of Attention

Our modern information ecosystems have fundamentally altered how we process information and direct our attention. The proliferation of social media, instant messaging, and constant connectivity has created an environment that actively shapes our cognitive patterns toward fragmentation and rapid context-switching. This isn’t a natural evolution — it’s a direct result of how our technological systems are designed and optimized.

The attention economy — algorithmic content delivery driven by engagement metrics — has created an environment that deliberately fragments our attention to maximize engagement (aka profit):

*   Rewards short, frequent interactions over sustained engagement
    
*   Prioritizes novelty and constant stimulation
    
*   Fragments information into ever-smaller units
    
*   Creates multiple competing demands for our attention
    

None of this is really new. We know all this.

Yet this technological reshaping of our attention patterns presents opportunities as well as challenges. While these systems have contributed to social fragmentation, polarization, and, ultimately, something that we could call cognitive fragmentation, understanding their influence allows us to design better alternatives — systems that embrace cognitive flexibility while preserving our capacity for deep focus when needed.

# From Deficit to Asset

The traditional narrative around scattered attention is one of deficit — a problem to be solved, a disorder to be treated, a modern ailment to be cured.

> **Attention is not a fixed resource to be optimized but a dynamic system that naturally oscillates between states of focus and diffusion. The challenge lies not in enforcing rigid patterns but in developing tools that support fluid transitions between these states.**

But what if we’re thinking about this entirely wrong? What if the ability to navigate multiple streams of thought, to rapidly switch contexts, to hold multiple perspectives simultaneously, isn’t a weakness but a kind of superpower? What if our current struggles aren’t with scattered attention itself, but with tools and environments that force us to work against our natural cognitive patterns?

The key insight isn’t that scattered attention is universally good, but rather that attention itself works more like a lens than a spotlight. What we need isn’t to force ourselves into rigid patterns of attention, but to develop the ability to fluidly adjust our “focus beam” — from narrow to wide and back again. Like a photographer who knows when to use a macro lens versus a wide-angle, different cognitive tasks benefit from different types of attention.

The key shift is in how we frame and support these cognitive patterns:

*   Instead of fighting against our tendency to make rapid associations, we can design tools that help us capture and explore these connections
    
*   Rather than forcing our thoughts into rigid structures, we can create flexible systems that adapt to our natural thought patterns
    
*   Instead of treating context-switching as a distraction, we can leverage it as a source of creative insight
    
*   Rather than seeing scattered attention as a modern curse, we can understand it as part of our cognitive toolkit
    

# Tools for Thought

The recognition of cognitive flexibility as a vital capability has profound implications for how we design our tools for thought.

> **The future of cognitive tools lies in their ability to mirror the natural rhythms of thought — supporting both the crystallization of ideas and their subsequent fragmentation, allowing meaning to exist in superposition until the moment of articulation.**

In the world of AI, we often hear that “context is king” — the more context a model has, the better it can understand and respond. The same is true for human cognition. Traditional approaches to productivity emphasize linear, single-focus attention, but this actually constrains the amount of context we can hold. When we rapidly switch between different threads of thought, we’re not just being “distracted” — we’re building a richer contextual understanding by weaving together multiple perspectives and insights.

Consider how traditional writing tools force us to slow our thinking down, making us go back and forth in one line of text, swapping out words, reordering sentences. This process of subvocalization — mentally speaking each word as we write — while useful for careful articulation, can interrupt our natural flow of thought. An interesting parallel appears in AI language models: they can compress text into abstract notations that other AIs can later reconstruct into coherent language. This suggests a possibility: what if we could capture thoughts in their raw, compressed form and only expand them when needed for communication?

To support this richer way of thinking, we need new architectural foundations that manifest in concrete interface patterns:

### **Context Aggregation & Dynamic Switching**

At the architectural level, we need systems that can automatically gather and maintain relevant context from multiple sources, creating a rich ambient awareness that informs our thinking without overwhelming it. Like a skilled research assistant who knows when to surface relevant information, these systems would help us maintain broader awareness while focusing on specific tasks.

This manifests in interfaces that treat context-switching not as disruption but as a core interaction pattern. Rather than minimizing task-switching, future interfaces should maintain multiple active threads of thought and their full context. Imagine an interface that doesn’t just allow you to switch between tasks, but actively helps you weave connections between them, turning apparent distractions into productive tangents.

### **Intent Inference & Fluid Movement**

We need tools that can recognize and adapt to different cognitive modes — whether we’re drilling deep into a specific topic or exploring broadly across domains. This isn’t just about tracking window states; it’s about understanding the trajectory of thought and adjusting the interface accordingly.

This enables fluid movement across detail levels — rather than forcing us to work at fixed levels of abstraction, interfaces should enable seamless zooming between high-level overview and granular detail. This mirrors how our minds naturally work — jumping from specific details to broad concepts and back again. The interface becomes a lens that can dynamically adjust its focal length to match our current thinking mode.

### **Pattern Recognition & Emergent Connections**

At their core, our tools need to be able to detect and maintain relationships between different pieces of information, allowing connections to emerge naturally from how we interact with our information rather than requiring explicit organization.

Instead of trying to eliminate “distracted” thinking, interfaces should help us capture and make sense of the connections our minds make during these moments. When your attention jumps between seemingly unrelated items, the system could track these patterns and help reveal the hidden logic in your thought process, transforming scattered attention into a tool for discovering non-obvious relationships.

### **State Preservation & Dynamic Reorganization**

We need mechanisms for capturing and preserving insights in their native form — whether they’re fully formed ideas or just fragmentary connections — without forcing them into predetermined structures.

This enables interfaces that allow information to be fluidly reorganized based on your current context and attention patterns. Like a living workspace that breathes with your thought process, it could expand areas that are currently relevant while maintaining peripheral awareness of related contexts.

These aren’t just theoretical possibilities — we’re already seeing early experiments with tools that embrace these principles, allowing us to work with our natural thought patterns rather than against them.

When we embrace scattered attention as an asset, entirely new possibilities emerge across different domains:

**Research and Analysis**: Imagine research environments that don’t force you to maintain a single line of inquiry, but help you explore multiple hypotheses in parallel. As your attention naturally bounces between different pieces of evidence, the system tracks these connections, helping you discover patterns you might miss if forced to think linearly.

**Creative Work:** Creative tools could embrace the way inspiration often comes from unexpected connections. Instead of organizing projects into rigid hierarchies, they could maintain fluid spaces where different ideas can collide and recombine, with the system helping to capture and preserve these serendipitous moments.

**Knowledge Management:** Rather than fighting against our tendency to context-switch, knowledge management systems could turn this into an advantage, using each switch as an opportunity to strengthen connections between different areas of knowledge and reveal new insights.

# Beyond the Deficit Model

> **The evolution of human-computer interaction must move beyond the false dichotomy of focus versus distraction. Instead, we should strive to create systems that embrace the full spectrum of cognitive states, recognizing that creativity often emerges from the interplay between order and chaos.**

This requires a fundamental rethinking of our relationship with technology and cognition. The challenge isn’t about choosing between focus and fragmentation — it’s about creating environments that support the natural rhythm of human thought, where both states are valued and leveraged effectively.

Artificial Intelligence has a crucial role to play in this evolution, not as a replacement for human thought, but as an enabler of more natural cognitive patterns. The future of human-AI interaction lies not in AI doing our thinking for us, but in it helping us think more naturally and effectively.

This vision naturally aligns with neuro-inclusive design principles, recognizing that what we call “scattered” attention may simply be a different, equally valid way of processing information. The goal isn’t to normalize one cognitive style over another, but to create environments where diverse cognitive patterns can thrive.

##### TL;DR

The scattered nature of modern attention isn’t simply a flaw to be fixed or an asset to be exploited — it’s part of a broader spectrum of cognitive states that we need to better understand and support. By designing tools and environments that work with our natural cognitive patterns, we can unlock new forms of creativity, productivity, and understanding.

The path forward isn’t to impose more structure or surrender to chaos, but to develop systems that help us find the natural patterns in our seemingly chaotic ways of thinking. The result could be a more humane and effective way of working with information — one that embraces the full spectrum of human cognitive capabilities.
